Neural Networks: How Machines Learn

Every concept introduced with an experiment first. Break things deliberately. Watch them fail. Fix one knob at a time. By the end you can look at any training curve and diagnose what is wrong without being told.

6.0 What Is a Neural Network?

In Chapter 3 you trained a Random Forest to predict machine failure. The Random Forest learned rules like "if temperature is above X and torque is above Y, flag as failure." It works well but the rules are fixed once training is done. A neural network learns differently — instead of rules, it learns adjustable numbers that it keeps tuning until its answers are correct.

Think of it like learning to cook. A recipe book gives you fixed rules: 200 degrees, 30 minutes, 2 cups of flour. A neural network is more like a chef who keeps tasting and adjusting — a pinch more salt here, two minutes less heat there — until the dish is right. The adjustments are the learning.

What is a neural network really?

A mathematical function with thousands of adjustable knobs called weights. Training is the process of turning those knobs until the function's output matches the correct answer for every training example.

What is it good for?

Neural networks shine when the relationship between inputs and outputs is too complex for simple rules. Recognizing a face, understanding speech, detecting objects in images. For simpler problems like the Chapter 3 failure dataset, Random Forest is often just as good and much simpler.

6.1 Anatomy of a Neuron

A neuron is the smallest building block of a neural network. Understanding one neuron completely means understanding the foundation of everything that follows.

Analogy — The exam scorer

Imagine scoring a student's exam performance using three things: homework average, midterm score, and attendance. You might decide homework matters most so you give it weight 0.5, midterm gets weight 0.4, and attendance gets only weight 0.1. You multiply each by its weight, add them up, and get a performance score. A neuron does exactly this — multiply each input by its weight and add them all up.

The only difference from the exam scorer is one extra step: after adding everything up, the neuron passes the result through an activation function. This is what makes neural networks powerful. Without it, no matter how many neurons you stack, the whole thing collapses into one simple weighted sum — and you are back to linear regression.

Step by step — what one neuron computes

Step 1 — Multiply each input by its weight

temperature × 0.8 = 0.72
vibration    × 0.3 = 0.21
speed        × -0.2 = -0.10

Step 2 — Add everything including the bias

z = 0.72 + 0.21 + (−0.10) + (−0.40) = 0.43

The bias −0.40 shifts the result. It is like a base score before any inputs are considered.

Step 3 — Pass through the activation function

sigmoid(0.43) = 0.606

Sigmoid squashes any number into a value between 0 and 1. Here: 60.6% confident something is abnormal.

6.2 ★ Experiment: The Perceptron Playground

You just saw one neuron compute a single number. Now let it make a decision: given two input features, classify a data point as red or blue. The neuron draws a boundary — everything on one side is red, everything on the other is blue.

The boundary is always a straight line. That is the fundamental limit of one neuron. For some problems a straight line is enough. For others it is completely useless. This experiment shows you both cases so you feel the limit before you understand why it exists.

What to do

  1. Run the cell with DATASET = "linear". Move W1, W2, and B until the line separates red from blue. This is solvable — a straight line works here.
  2. Change to DATASET = "xor". Now try to separate them. Spend two minutes genuinely trying. You cannot do it. A straight line will always leave some red on the blue side.
  3. Change to DATASET = "circles". Same problem — the boundary you need is a circle, not a line.

Question

After trying XOR: what shape of boundary would actually work? A curve? Two lines? This question has a real answer and lesson 6.3 gives it to you.

⚠️
One neuron — one straight line

A single neuron can only draw a straight line. XOR and circles are not linearly separable — no straight line can divide them. This is why we need multiple neurons in multiple layers.

Why does this matter?

When you built the failure predictor in Chapter 3, the data was roughly linearly separable — normal operation and failure formed groups that a line could mostly separate. That is why simple algorithms worked well. Neural networks become valuable when your data looks more like the XOR or circles problem — when the pattern you are trying to find cannot be captured by a straight line. Real robotics data (camera images, audio signals, complex sensor combinations) almost always looks like circles, not like two clean blobs.

6.3 From One Neuron to Many: Layers

The perceptron playground proved one neuron cannot solve XOR. But what if you used three neurons? The trick is that each neuron draws its own line — and three lines together can carve out any shape you want.

Analogy — The committee decision

One judge gives one opinion. Three judges can together make a more nuanced decision — one might care about speed, another about accuracy, a third about style. Their combined vote can capture patterns no single judge could see. A hidden layer is a committee of neurons voting together.

What a layer actually does

Each neuron in the hidden layer gets the same inputs but has different weights — so each neuron learns to notice a different aspect of the data. The outputs of all those neurons become the inputs to the next layer. By the time data reaches the output layer, it has been transformed so many times that previously impossible separations become simple.

Counting the knobs

Every connection between neurons is one weight — one knob to tune. Every neuron also has one bias. The total number of these knobs is the number of parameters. More parameters means more capacity to learn complex patterns, but also more data needed and more risk of memorizing instead of learning.

For count_params([3, 4, 4, 1]) — walk through it:

Layer 1 (3→4): Each of the 3 inputs connects to each of the 4 neurons. That is 3×4 = 12 weights. Each of the 4 neurons also has 1 bias. Total: 16 parameters.

Grand total: 41 parameters. This network can be trained on a laptop in seconds. YOLO26n has 3.5 million. GPT-4 has roughly 1 trillion. The math is the same — just way more of it.

6.4 How Networks Learn: Gradient Descent

Networks have tens to millions of weights. Training has to find the right value for all of them simultaneously. How? Not by trying every combination — there are too many. Instead, training makes small improvements over and over until the network is good enough. This process is called gradient descent.

Analogy — The blindfolded hiker

You are blindfolded on a hilly landscape. Your goal is to find the lowest valley. You cannot see anything — but you can feel the slope under your feet. At each step you feel which direction slopes downward and take one step that way. After enough steps you reach a valley. Gradient descent is this process, but the landscape has millions of dimensions instead of two.

What is the loss?

The height in the hiking analogy is the loss — a number that measures how wrong the network is. When the network predicts 0.9 failure probability and the true label is 0 (no failure), that is a large loss. When it predicts 0.05 and the label is 0, the loss is small. Training minimizes this number.

The most common loss for classification is called cross-entropy. For regression it is mean squared error — the average of (prediction − true value)² across all training examples. You do not need to derive these. Just know that loss = how wrong the network is, and training makes it smaller.

The five steps of one training iteration

Step 1 — Forward pass

Feed one batch of training examples (say, 32 rows) through the network from left to right. Every neuron computes its weighted sum and activation. The final layer produces a prediction for each of the 32 examples.

Step 2 — Compute loss

Compare the 32 predictions to the 32 true labels. Compute the average error. This single number tells you how wrong the network is right now.

Step 3 — Backward pass (backpropagation)

Figure out which weights caused the most error. Calculus tells us how much each weight contributed to the loss — this is the gradient. The gradient points in the direction that increases loss. We want to go the opposite direction.

Step 4 — Update weights

Move every weight a small step in the direction that reduces loss.

new weight = old weight − learning rate × gradient

The learning rate controls how big the step is. Too large and the network overshoots. Too small and training takes forever.

Step 5 — Repeat

Do this for every batch in the training set. When you have gone through every batch once, that is one epoch. Training runs for many epochs until the loss stops improving.

6.6 The Three Knobs: Learning Rate, Epochs, Architecture

Every neural network training run has three settings you choose before anything starts. These are the three knobs. Getting them right is more art than science — but you can develop intuition by understanding what each one does and watching what happens when you get it wrong. The next lesson is entirely about that.

Learning Rate

Controls the step size during gradient descent. Too small and training crawls — it might take 10,000 epochs to converge. Too large and the network overshoots the minimum, bounces around, or diverges entirely. You just saw this in lesson 6.5. The safe default with the Adam optimizer is 0.001. In the next experiment you will use plain gradient descent where 0.1 is the sweet spot.

Default: 0.001 (Adam)

Epochs

One epoch = one complete pass through all your training data. Too few epochs and the network has not seen enough examples to learn the pattern — this is called underfitting. Too many epochs and the network memorizes the training examples instead of learning the general pattern — this is called overfitting. You will see both of these failures in lesson 6.9.

Typical range: 10–500

Architecture

How many layers and how many neurons per layer. A network that is too small does not have enough parameters to represent the pattern in the data. A network that is too large has so many parameters it memorizes every training example instead of generalizing. A common starting architecture for simple problems is two hidden layers with 16 or 32 neurons each. You will build this in the next lesson.

Start: 2 hidden layers, 16–32 neurons

6.8 Activation Functions: Why Non-Linearity Matters

Every neuron applies an activation function after its weighted sum. Without this step, stacking neurons does nothing useful — the whole network collapses to one big linear function regardless of how many layers you add. The activation function is what gives neural networks their power.

Sigmoid

f(z) = 1 / (1 + e⁻ᶻ)

Output: 0 to 1. Good for the output layer of binary classifiers. Problem: vanishing gradient — for large |z| the gradient shrinks near zero, so early layers stop learning.

ReLU

f(z) = max(0, z)

Output: 0 to ∞. The default choice for hidden layers. Fast and simple. Problem: dying ReLU — neurons that always output 0 stop contributing entirely.

Tanh

f(z) = (eᶻ − e⁻ᶻ) / (eᶻ + e⁻ᶻ)

Output: −1 to 1. Zero-centered version of sigmoid. Often works better in practice for hidden layers than sigmoid.

💡
Practical rule

Hidden layers: use ReLU. Output for binary classification: sigmoid. Output for multi-class: softmax. Output for regression: no activation (linear output).

6.9 Overfitting and How to Fight It

Every neural network can fail in two opposite directions. Too little training and the network never learns the pattern. Too much training and the network memorizes the training data instead of generalizing. These are underfitting and overfitting.

Underfitting

Both train and val loss plateau high. The network has not learned the pattern. Causes: too small a network, too few epochs, learning rate too small. Fix: increase architecture, train longer.

Good Fit

Both losses decrease and level off together. This is what you want. Train loss is slightly lower than val loss — that small gap is normal.

Overfitting

Train loss keeps decreasing but val loss turns back up. The hallmark: the gap between them grows over time. The network is memorizing, not learning.

Three ways to fight overfitting

Dropout

During training, randomly set some neurons to zero on each forward pass. Forces the network to learn redundant representations. In Keras: model.add(Dropout(0.3)) — 30% of neurons zeroed each batch.

Early stopping

Stop training when validation loss stops improving. Keras: EarlyStopping(patience=10) stops after 10 epochs with no improvement and restores the best weights.

More data

Always the best cure when you can get it. More diverse examples force the network to generalize rather than memorize.

6.11 Reading Training Curves

A training curve is the first thing you look at when something goes wrong. Learn to read it and you can diagnose most problems without running more experiments.

Symptom Diagnosis Fix
Loss stays flat from epoch 1 LR too small or bad initialization Increase LR or use Adam
Loss spikes or goes to NaN LR too large Reduce LR by 10×
Train loss falls, val loss rises after some epochs Overfitting Add Dropout, early stopping, or reduce network size
Both train and val loss plateau high Underfitting More epochs, larger network, or different architecture
Val loss lower than train loss Normal with Dropout active at train time Expected — no action needed
Train accuracy 100%, val accuracy low Severe overfitting (memorization) Much more data or much smaller network
Both losses very noisy throughout Batch size too small or LR too large Increase batch size to 32 or 64

6.12 When to Use Neural Networks

Neural networks are not always the right tool. Understanding when not to use one is as important as knowing how to build one.

Do you have >10,000 training examples?
NO → Try Random Forest first. If you must use NN: keep it small and use heavy regularization.
YES → Continue

Use a neural network when…

Your data is images, audio, or raw text. Your dataset is large (thousands of examples or more). Simpler models have already been tried and underperform. You do not need to explain individual predictions.

Prefer Random Forest or XGBoost when…

Your data is tabular (rows and columns). Your dataset is small to medium. You need to explain predictions to stakeholders. You want a fast baseline with minimal tuning.
